# 🎬 Who Profits From School Child Labor — Expanded YouTube Narration

**5–6.5 minute documentary-style narration**
**Formatted for ElevenLabs voiceover + timestamps + B-roll + emphasis cues**

---

## INTRO — THE HARVEST QUESTION (0:00–0:50)

**[VOICE: slow, serious, controlled | pace 0.86]**

School is not optional.

**[PAUSE 0.7s]**

It never was.

For over a hundred years, children have been legally required to give their time to an institution they did not choose.

Not for pay.

Not by choice.

**[PAUSE 1.0s]**

So the question is simple:

**Who benefits from that?**

Because once you follow the money, you do not just find schools.

**[PAUSE 0.6s]**

You find corporate interests, corruption, and the enslavement of children for profit.

**[B-ROLL: empty classroom, security camera in hallway, rows of desks, slow push-in]**

It is system where time becomes a product, pressure becomes normal, and childhood becomes input.

And once that system exists, people start depending on it.

People need it to continue.

---

## SECTION 1 — COMPULSION CREATES A GUARANTEED MARKET (0:50–2:10)

**[VOICE: neutral documentary tone | pace 0.94]**

School is not a normal market.

You do not choose it.

You are required to attend.

By 1918, every U.S. state had compulsory attendance laws.

That creates something unusual:

**[PAUSE 0.8s]**

A guaranteed population.

Millions of children who must show up, every day, whether they want to or not.

**[B-ROLL: archival school footage, black-and-white classroom photos, school bus arrivals]**

And once attendance is mandatory, a funding pipeline forms around it.

Districts buy curriculum, tests, devices, transportation, meals, buildings, software, and internet access.

In some places, funding tracks attendance.

So more time in classrooms can mean more money for vendors and staffing.

**[VOICE: slower, emphasis]**

Time becomes revenue.

And when the school day ends, it often does not really end.

Homework reaches into nights, weekends, and sleep.

That is not just rhetoric. Specific studies have documented the damage.

**[B-ROLL: child doing homework at night, clock passing midnight, tired face in lamp light]**

**Yu and colleagues, *"Homework, sleep insufficiency and adolescent neurobehavioral problems"*** in the ***Journal of Affective Disorders* (2023)**, tied heavy homework to sleep loss and worse emotional and behavioral outcomes.

**Yeo and colleagues, *"Associations of time spent on homework or studying with nocturnal sleep behavior and depression symptoms in adolescents from Singapore"*** in ***Sleep Health* (2020)**, found that longer study hours meant less time in bed, and in that high-pressure system, long study hours were also linked to higher depression symptoms. On weekends, that link to depression still remained even after the researchers accounted for sleep.

And a large longitudinal study led by University College London, **Sullivan et al., *"The association between academic pressure and adolescent depressive symptoms and self-harm: a longitudinal, prospective study in England"*** in ***The Lancet Child & Adolescent Health* (2026)**, found that pressure to achieve at school was linked to higher depressive symptoms and increased risk of self-harm, with effects persisting into adulthood.

**[PAUSE 0.8s]**

But none of that extra time is paid.

It is required.

So the system does not just take hours inside the building.

It reaches into the child’s home life too.

---

## SECTION 2 — SCALE, PAYROLL, AND THE VENDOR STACK (2:10–3:45)

**[VOICE: informative, slightly faster | pace 0.98]**

Now follow the money.

Public K through 12 education in the United States takes in roughly nine hundred billion dollars a year.

A huge share goes to payroll.

Another massive share goes to buildings, land, equipment, and infrastructure.

**[B-ROLL: budget documents, school construction, cafeteria lines, school laptops, telecom wiring]**

And then there is the wider vendor stack:

construction companies

technology vendors

testing contractors

food service providers

telecom infrastructure

consultants

software subscriptions

maintenance contracts

transportation fleets

Entire industries plug into compulsory schooling and benefit from its scale.

Real examples make that visible.

In Texas, a four-year statewide testing contract worth hundreds of millions of dollars moved from one company to another, but the testing mandate stayed.

In Los Angeles Unified, a half-billion-dollar iPad and curriculum deal went to Apple and Pearson.

The companies can change.

The vendor names can change.

**[VOICE: slower, emphasis]**

But the guaranteed demand remains.

And scholars have described that broader trend directly.

**Stephen Ball**, in ***"Globalised, Commodified and Privatised: Current International Trends in Education and Education Policy"***, and ***"Privatising Education, Privatising Education Policy, Privatising Educational Research"***, describes schooling as a field of commodities, contracts, and commercialization.

That matters because markets like these cause those involved to have a vested interest in continuing them.

---

## SECTION 3 — LOBBYING, EXAMS, AND THE ANXIETY MARKET (3:45–5:30)

**[VOICE: sharper, more forceful | pace 0.95]**

The money is not only in contracts.

It is also in policy.

It is in lobbying.

It is in deciding how much testing children face, which exams remain central, and which institutions keep getting paid.

**[B-ROLL: capitol building, lobbyists walking, exam booklets, scantron sheets, students waiting nervously]**

A **2015 analysis by the Center for Media and Democracy** reported that **Pearson, ETS, Houghton Mifflin Harcourt, and McGraw-Hill** together spent more than twenty million dollars lobbying state and federal officials from 2009 to 2014, while the expanded testing regime fueled a nearly two-billion-dollar annual testing business.

In its 2024 tax filing, the **College Board** reported over one billion dollars in revenue, while federal lobbying disclosures showed the organization lobbying on education appropriations and education assessments.

So once schools convince families that scores are central, a secondary market appears around the pressure.

Test prep.

Private tutoring.

Coaching.

Cram programs.

**[B-ROLL: tutoring centers, SAT prep ads, kids in cram-school style classrooms, stressed parents]**

**Edward Kim and coauthors, in *"Kumon In: The Recent, Rapid Rise of Private Tutoring Centers"***, found that the number of private tutoring centers in the United States more than tripled, from about three thousand in 1997 to about ten thousand by 2022.

So the sequence becomes brutally clear:

Schools create pressure.

Tests formalize it.

Then businesses sell families a way through it.

**[VOICE: slower]**

Pressure becomes a product.

And the political structure around schooling does not belong only to corporations.

Teachers' unions also wield organized labor power over pay, staffing, and politics.

**Joshua Cowen and Katharine Strunk**, in ***"How Do Teachers' Unions Influence Education Policy? What We Know and What We Need to Learn"***, describe the main channels of influence as collective bargaining, elections, and lobbying.

**Michael Hartney and Patrick Flavin**, in ***"From the Schoolhouse to the Statehouse: Teacher Union Political Activism and U.S. State Education Reform Policy"*** in ***State Politics & Policy Quarterly* (2011)**, show teacher-union political activity shaping whether states adopt reforms like school choice and performance pay.

Whatever side someone takes on unions, the structural point is still there:

Workers have organized representation.

Vendors have contracts.

Testing firms have lobbying budgets.

Prep companies have paying customers.

**[PAUSE 0.8s]**

The student has none of that.

No union.

No ability to strike.

No seat at the bargaining table.

But the student is still the one required to participate.

---

## OUTRO — EXTRACTION IS NOT AN ACCIDENT (5:30–6:35)

**[VOICE: slow, reflective, darker | pace 0.84]**

So step back and look at the whole thing.

A system where attendance is mandatory.

A system where time drives funding.

A system where pressure drives spending.

A system where adults negotiate around the institution, profit from it, lobby around it, and build businesses on top of it.

**[B-ROLL: empty hallway, fluorescent lights, exhausted student on bus, sunset over school fence]**

And where does the cost go?

Not into the contracts.

Not into the balance sheets.

It shows up in lost sleep.

In stress.

In depression.

In self-harm.

In years of childhood that do not come back.

John Holt, in ***How Children Fail***, put the scam plainly:

> "It begins to look as if the test-examination-marks business is a gigantic racket, the purpose of which is to enable students, teachers, and schools to take part in a joint pretense that the students know everything they are supposed to know, when in fact they know only a small part of it—if any at all."
>
>

**[PAUSE 1.0s]**

That is not just bad teaching.

It is a structure of incentives.

A structure built on time, compliance, pressure, and continuation.

And once people and corporations depend on those things for their livelihoods and profits, they rarely give them up willingly.

**[FINAL LINE | slower, quiet]**

The incentives cause the powerful to enslave children in a prison, and official story says the child should be grateful for the lock.

John Taylor Gatto, in ***Dumbing Us Down***, said:

> "School is a twelve-year jail sentence where bad habits are the only curriculum truly learned. I teach school and win awards doing it. I should know."
