### **THE MYTH OF SOCIAL MOBILITY THROUGH SCHOOL**

#### _School does not raise income or lift people out of poverty._

---

# **INTRO (0:00–0:45)**

**NARRATOR (V.O.)**  
School is promoted as the pathway out of poverty.  
Study hard, get good grades, earn a degree, and you’ll rise into a higher income level.

But decades of economic research show this promise is false.  
School does not reliably increase income, does not reduce poverty, and does not create upward mobility.

---

# **SECTION 1 — EDUCATION AND INCOME ARE ONLY WEAKLY LINKED (0:45–3:30)**

**NARRATOR (V.O.)**  
Harvard and Stanford’s _Equality of Opportunity Project_, analyzing more than **20 million U.S. tax records**, found that **parent income predicts adult income far more accurately than education does**.  
Two students with the same degree can end up in completely different income brackets because schooling has little influence on long-term earnings.

A major review in the _Journal of Economic Literature_ by Bowles, Gintis, and Osborne found that **years of schooling explain only about 10–15% of income differences** across individuals.  
The majority is determined by factors outside of education: family resources, networks, geography, opportunity structures, and early life conditions.

The **Federal Reserve’s Household Economics Survey** shows that many college graduates—especially first-generation and working-class students—**see no meaningful financial improvement** after earning degrees.  
Many simply remain in the same economic bracket they were born into.

Internationally, the **OECD’s Education at a Glance** reports that the **earnings premium for degrees has declined** across many advanced economies.  
In countries like Italy, Spain, and Greece, graduates now earn **roughly the same** as non-graduates because of labor-market saturation.

Across all these studies, the pattern is consistent:  
**education does not reliably increase income.**

---

# **SECTION 2 — SCHOOLING HAS NOT RAISED INCOME IN THE DEVELOPING WORLD (3:30–6:00)**

**NARRATOR (V.O.)**  
If schooling lifted people out of poverty, the effect would appear everywhere.  
But research from Africa, South Asia, and Latin America shows that **expanding access to education does not increase income** for poor populations.

Lant Pritchett’s World Bank study, _Where Has All the Education Gone?_, found no relationship between increases in national schooling levels and economic growth or higher earnings.

Harvard’s Center for International Development found that in Malawi, **additional schooling did not improve wages or productivity**, because hiring is driven by networks and local opportunity—not credentials.

World Bank labor research in Nigeria showed that university graduates from poor families earn **almost the same** as those who never attended university, because formal sector jobs are scarce.

Zambia, Kenya, Zimbabwe, and South Africa show the same outcome:  
**more schooling does not change the economic trajectory of people born into poverty.**

---

# **SECTION 3 — CREDENTIAL INFLATION (FINAL, CLEAN VERSION)**

**NARRATOR (V.O.)**  
Another reason schooling no longer increases income is simple saturation.

Data from the **U.S. Census Bureau** shows that the share of adults with a college degree has more than doubled since the 1970s. College completion expanded across the population, but income levels did not rise with it.

At the same time, data from the **Bureau of Labor Statistics** shows that many occupations employing large numbers of college graduates have experienced little or no real wage growth over decades. As degrees became more common, earnings in those jobs remained flat.

When nearly everyone has a degree, **having one does not increase earnings**.
---

# **SECTION 4 — DEBT WITHOUT ECONOMIC RETURN (7:15–8:30)**

**NARRATOR (V.O.)**  
Tuition has risen over **180% since the 1980s**, while wages have barely changed.  
Student debt in the United States is now over **$1.77 trillion**.

Independent analysis by Third Way shows that **half of all college programs fail to deliver a positive financial return** within five years of graduation.

Schooling becomes a financial burden, not a pathway to higher income.

---

# **SECTION 5 — NETWORKS DETERMINE INCOME MORE THAN SCHOOLING (8:30–9:45)**

**NARRATOR (V.O.)**  
LinkedIn hiring analysis shows that **most jobs are filled through referrals**, not online applications.

Research by Lauren Rivera finds that employers rely heavily on **connections, personal familiarity, and cultural fit**, not on degrees.

Income today is shaped by **opportunity access**, not classroom credentials.

---

# **SECTION 6 — WHY SCHOOL DOES NOT CREATE MOBILITY (9:45–11:00)**

_(This is the only surviving Section 6 content, with supporting studies.)_

**NARRATOR (V.O.)**  
Finally, poor students spend 12–16 years in a system that does not teach the practical economic skills that actually improve income—skills such as entrepreneurship, financial competence, negotiation, or technical expertise. Research from the **OECD’s Skills for Jobs** database and **Harvard’s Graduate School of Education** shows that traditional schooling is poorly aligned with the skills demanded by modern labor markets. As a result, students from low-income backgrounds leave school with the same limited economic opportunities they started with—only older, and often carrying debt.

---

# **SECTION 7 — WHAT ACTUALLY CREATES MOBILITY TODAY (11:00–12:00)**

**NARRATOR (V.O.)**  
Modern income mobility comes from skills, not schooling.

Companies like Google, IBM, and Tesla have eliminated degree requirements for many roles.  
_Harvard Business Review_ reports that **skills-based hiring is replacing degree-based hiring** across industries.

Upward mobility now comes from:

- real-world capability
    
- practical skills
    
- entrepreneurship
    
- technical competence
    
- and access to opportunity
    

—not from traditional education.

---

# **OUTRO (12:00–13:00)**

**NARRATOR (V.O.)**  
School does not raise income.  
School does not reduce poverty.  
School does not create upward mobility.

Economic outcomes today depend on skills and opportunity—not on a system designed around outdated assumptions.

**TITLE CARD:**  
School is a waste of life.

**END.**
