# Schooling Did Not Help Poverty in Africa

The case for schooling in Africa has been:

That education reduces poverty.  
That income rises with years spent in classrooms.  
That school is the ladder out of economic hardship.

But when we look at income, employment, self-reliance, and the results of Western education, these promises do not hold.

At both the **individual** and **societal** level, formal schooling in Africa has not only failed to eliminate poverty — it has produced deeper and more widespread poverty.

---

## Life Before School: Economic Coherence, Not Wealth

Before Western schooling, Africa was not wealthy, but it was **economically coherent**, meaning production, skill transmission, labor, and social support were integrated into daily life. This allowed most people to meet their needs without dependence on formal employment or distant markets.

Villages functioned as complete economic systems rooted in land access, kinship, and reciprocal obligation. Children learned by participating — farming, herding, fishing, building, trading, healing — rather than waiting for jobs.

Economic anthropologist **Polly Hill**, studying West African rural economies, described societies in which nearly all household members engaged in multiple livelihoods. Wage labor was marginal, involuntary unemployment was rare, and economic participation was embedded in everyday life. Poverty existed, but **systematic idleness and mass joblessness did not**.

This was not unique. **Marshall Sahlins** described hunter-gatherer societies as “the original affluent society,” meeting their needs with limited labor and enjoying more leisure time than modern populations. Research by **James Woodburn** and **Richard Lee** documented African small-scale economies organized around shared access to food, land, and labor, where chronic hunger, homelessness, and social abandonment were rare because survival was secured through kinship and reciprocal exchange rather than wage competition.

Historian **John Iliffe**, in *The African Poor*, reached a similar conclusion at a continental scale. Pre-colonial African societies experienced hardship and famine, but they generally lacked large, permanent classes of unemployed, landless, or chronically destitute people because access to land, work, and social support remained widespread.

When formal schooling removed children from these productive roles for long periods, many entered economies unable to absorb them. Contemporary research such as the **ILO’s Global Employment Trends for Youth** and the **World Bank’s Africa’s Pulse** documents persistent youth unemployment, underemployment, and growing urban poverty despite rising schooling levels. Widespread youth unemployment and permanent urban poverty are common now in Africa, but were largely absent before. 

Schooling did not improve upon traditional systems.  
It dismantled them by removing generations of youth from productive participation.

---

## Schooling Did Not Eliminate Poverty — It Restructured It

At both the **individual** and **societal** level, the failure of schooling is structural.

School delays entry into real economic life without providing land, tools, capital, or useful skills. Graduates are released into economies dominated by unemployment and informal labor. At the individual level, poverty is often not removed but **postponed**.

In Ghana, **Marfo and Okireku (2015)** found that secondary-school graduates frequently earned no more — and sometimes less — than peers who entered informal work earlier. In urban Ethiopia, **Serneels (2007)** showed that unemployment was concentrated among relatively educated young men queuing for scarce formal jobs.

At the societal level, Western schooling did not arrive to complement village economies; it replaced them. By keeping children in school for long periods, it disrupted the transmission of farming, craft, trade, and economic responsibility to the next generation.

In return, schooling promised income. But decades of research show that promise was not met. Economist **Lant Pritchett**, in *Where Has All the Education Gone?*, demonstrated that although schooling expanded rapidly across the Global South, job creation and earnings did not. More education did not result in more hiring or higher incomes for most people.

The **World Bank**, in *Youth Employment in Sub-Saharan Africa* (2014), similarly acknowledged that schooling expanded far faster than employment. Enrollment rose. Years of schooling increased. Poverty remained.

After independence in the 1960s, African cities filled with school graduates without work — a condition researchers described as **educated unemployment**. Poverty did not disappear; it changed form. It moved from rural households that met most needs through farming, herding, local trade, and shared family work, to urban populations dependent on cash wages they could not reliably earn.

When traditional survival systems were dismantled and no new livelihoods emerged, many were pushed into dangerous and unstable work. Research including:
*Education, Training, and Livelihoods in Artisanal Mining*
*Poverty Traps in Small-Scale Mining Communities*
*Education and Employment in Artisanal and Small-Scale Mining* 
show that formal schooling often fails to translate into stable employment, with many educated youth ultimately entering hazardous informal sectors such as mining.

Schooling did not eliminate poverty.  
At the individual level, it delayed it.  
At the societal level, it dismantled existing means of survival without replacing them.  
At the political level, schooling has kept populations poor, controlled, and easily exploitable by governments and corporations.

## Education as Alienation: What African Thinkers Warned

African thinkers warned that schooling would do exactly this.

**Julius Nyerere**, the first president of Tanzania, argued that education must be tied to production and self-reliance rather than job-seeking.  
“Education must be for self-reliance… not for the pursuit of salaried employment alone.”  
“The colonial system was designed to produce clerks and administrators, not self-reliant citizens.”

Scholars and activists **Steve Biko**, **Frantz Fanon**, **Claude Ake**, and **Ali A. Mazrui** argued that colonial and post-colonial schooling reshaped minds and aspirations without building self-reliance. They warned schooling produced obedience, alienation, and dependence rather than liberation.

**Patrice Lumumba**, the **first Prime Minister of the Democratic Republic of the Congo** said Western education trained obedience and erased African history rather than building self-rule — “**a people without a history is a people without a soul**.” For rejecting imperial control after independence, Lumumba was removed and executed through a CIA backed coup.

Across these thinkers, the warning is consistent: schooling removes people from productive life and replaces self-reliance with dependence.

## Chinweizu: Schooling as the Weapon

Nigerian writer **Chinweizu Ibekwe** argued Western education in Africa is not a way to help societies improve their lives, but a system that weakens self-reliance by teaching people to abandon the skills and work that traditionally sustained them.

He wrote:

> “The West has educated Africans  
> out of self-trust,  
> out of confidence in their own cultures,  
> and into the role of imitators and consumers of foreign models.”

He argued schooling is a tool of cultural genocide :

> “Western education in Africa has produced a class of Africans  
> who are strangers to their own societies  
> and agents of Western interests,  
> whether they know it or not.”

At the level of thought itself, Chinweizu was unambiguous:

> “Colonial education did not train Africans to think —  
> it trained them to accept Western definitions of reality.”

 Children are pulled away from traditional skills, and are instead taught to measure their intelligence and self-worth through their grades and participation in wage slavery. They are trained to reject the African systems which sustained life and prevented mass, permanent poverty.

For Chinweizu, the impact was clear:

> “The most devastating weapon of imperialism  
> is not the gun or the bomb —  
> it is the school that teaches the conquered  
> to despise themselves.”
